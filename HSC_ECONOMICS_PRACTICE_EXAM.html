✍️ Model Structure — What to Write in Each Paragraph

INTRODUCTION (6–8 lines)

  • Define: Economic policy = macro (demand management via monetary/fiscal) + micro (supply-side structural reform)
  • Key concept: STABILITY (price stability, full employment, sustainable growth)
  • 1 stat: Inflation peaked 7.8% (Dec 2022), fell, but rose AGAIN to 4.6% — forcing RBA to reverse in 2026
  • Global: Middle East fuel prices, US trade policy uncertainty
  • Balanced thesis: "While the coordinated policy stance initially achieved a 'soft landing,' the RBA's forced reversal in 2026 — raising rates three consecutive times after premature easing in 2025 — reveals the limitations of demand management amid persistent global supply shocks."
  • [STIMULUS]: "As Source 1 illustrates, the cash rate's U-shaped trajectory demonstrates the difficulty of calibrating policy amid volatile global conditions."

BP1 — MONETARY POLICY (Biggest paragraph — your showpiece)

  • Topic sentence: RBA's monetary policy = primary instrument for achieving price stability through the transmission mechanism
  • Phase 1 (Tightening 2022–23): Raised 0.10% → 4.35% (13 consecutive rises). Response to 7.8% CPI inflation driven by demand-pull (COVID savings) + cost-push (supply chain disruptions, energy shocks)
  • Effectiveness of Phase 1: Inflation fell 7.8% → within 2–3% target band by mid-2025. Cash-flow channel potent — mortgage repayments ↑~$1,200/month. Savings ratio collapsed 8.3% → 0.6%
  • Phase 2 (Easing 2025): Cut 3 times (Feb, May, Aug) down to 3.60%. RBA believed inflation had been beaten and shifted to supporting growth
  • Phase 3 (FORCED REVERSAL 2026): Inflation picked up H2 2025 due to Middle East fuel prices and tight labour market. RBA raised 3 consecutive times back to 4.35%. March vote split 5–4 — indicates uncertainty
  • RBA SOMP May 2026: Forecasts inflation peaking 4.8%, GDP halving to 1.3%, unemployment rising to 4.7%
  • What this reveals: Premature easing in 2025 → forced reversal in 2026 = forecasting difficulty. Blunt instrument — can't target specific causes like fuel prices. 12–18 month transmission lag creates uncertainty
  • STIMULUS: "As Source 1 demonstrates, the cash rate's complete reversal from 3.60% back to 4.35% confirms Source 2's observation that global uncertainty 'continues to be a big influence' beyond domestic policy control."

BP2 — FISCAL POLICY

  • Topic sentence: Fiscal policy complemented then DIVERGED from monetary stance — creating critical coordination tensions
  • Evolution: COVID deficit –$134.2bn (2020–21) → surpluses $22.1bn + $9.3bn (2022–24, first back-to-back since 2007–08) → deficit –$42.1bn (2025–26)
  • Consolidation mechanism: Surpluses achieved through bracket creep + elevated commodity revenue — NOT deliberate spending restraint. Supported RBA's disinflation objective
  • Recent pivot: Returned to deficit, government shifted to cost-of-living support: $250 Working Australians Tax Offset (13.3m workers)
  • KEY CONFLICT — Working at Cross-Purposes: Fiscal now EXPANSIONARY (widening deficits, tax cuts, spending) while monetary CONTRACTIONARY (3 rate hikes in 2026). These work AGAINST each other — fiscal adds demand while RBA tries to reduce it. Reduces effectiveness of both
  • Other fiscal pressures: Defence 3% of GDP by 2033 ($53bn/10yrs), NDIS, aged care = structural spending growth beyond short-term policy cycles
  • STIMULUS: "Source 2 claims 'spending restraint' yet Source 1 shows deficits widening from –$26.9bn to –$42.1bn — suggesting fiscal consolidation abandoned, potentially complicating RBA's contractionary task."

BP3 — MICROECONOMIC POLICY

  • Topic sentence: Micro reform addresses supply-side structural constraints — key concept = PRODUCTIVITY
  • The problem: Productivity growth at 60-year low. Only sustainable long-run source of rising living standards. Macro policy CANNOT fix supply-side deficiencies
  • 2026–27 Budget measures: $10.2bn/yr compliance cost reduction | Permanent 2-year loss carry-back (companies <$1bn) | Expanded venture capital tax incentives | Permanent $20k instant asset write-off | R&D Tax Incentive reforms (July 2028)
  • Labour market: FWC annual wage review, $316.1m employment services investment. Skills transition (manufacturing → services/knowledge sectors)
  • Housing (critical supply-side): ↑4% dwelling investment, $2bn enabling infrastructure (65,000 new homes). Addresses structural undersupply that monetary policy CAN'T fix
  • Theory: Shifts LRAS rightward → enables higher potential output without inflationary pressure
  • Why it matters NOW: With cash rate at 4.35% and fiscal adding demand, micro reform = ONLY viable pathway to non-inflationary growth

BP4 — LIMITATIONS & CONFLICTS

  • Topic sentence: Despite partial success, significant structural limitations constrain policy effectiveness
  • 1. Time lags + forecasting failure: 12–18 month transmission lag. 2025 rate cuts were premature → inflation returned → forced 2026 reversal. March 5–4 vote shows Board uncertainty. Inherent difficulty of forward-looking policy
  • 2. Phillips Curve trade-off: Unemployment 3.5% → 4.3% → 4.7% (forecast). GDP 2.6% → 1.3% (halving). Per capita GDP fell 6 consecutive quarters. Population growth (2.4%) masks genuine decline in living standards
  • 3. Global conditions beyond domestic control: Middle East → fuel prices ("big influence" — Chalmers). US trade policy "back in the news" — volatility. Oil price shocks persistent. Domestic policy cannot insulate from exogenous shocks
  • 4. Fiscal-monetary conflict: Fiscal EXPANSIONARY while monetary CONTRACTIONARY = working at cross-purposes. Political incentives (elections) bias fiscal toward spending. Reduces effectiveness of both instruments
  • 5. Structural issues macro can't fix: Housing shortfall (200,000+). Skills shortages (healthcare, construction). Productivity stagnation (60-year low). These require decades of sustained microeconomic reform
  • STIMULUS: "Source 2 acknowledges global forces 'beyond control' — the RBA's forced reversal visible in Source 1 is direct evidence that exogenous constraints override domestic policy settings."

CONCLUSION (2–3 sentences — STOP HERE)

  • Australia's macroeconomic policies achieved initial success in restoring price stability — reducing inflation from 7.8% toward the 2–3% target band without precipitating recession.
  • However, the RBA's forced reversal in 2026 (three consecutive rate hikes after premature easing in 2025) demonstrates the limitations of demand management amid persistent global supply shocks, while fiscal and monetary policy now work at cross-purposes.
  • Structural constraints — including historically low productivity, housing undersupply, and skills shortages — remain beyond the capacity of demand management to resolve, necessitating sustained microeconomic reform as a complement to counter-cyclical policy.
